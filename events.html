Where: Virtual
When: 18 March 2021, 12-1 PM EST/EDT

LTU Physics Alumni Patrick Carzon and Brendan Koch will be giving talks on their research, field of study, and their experiences in graduate school.

P. Carzon: Medical physics is a booming field in today's world; however, it is often hidden "behind the scenes". The medical physics field deals a lot with cancer treatment and has a substantial impact on the medical field even without necessarily dealing with patients directly. From patient treatment planning to medical image processing to imaging modality development, medical physics is a very specific branch of physics that has a very broad scope of applications. If you enjoy physics and are fascinated with the medical field, then medical physics has the best of both worlds for your taste.

B. Koch: Using our understanding of the electromagnetic force, it is possible to study the properties and structure of the atom, nucleus, and proton. Trying to look at more fundamental structures, like quarks, becomes difficult due to the strong force which holds them together. Heavy ion collisions can be used to break down atoms to their most fundamental parts, quarks and gluons, and create a plasma that allows us to study their properties. This Quark Gluon Plasma (QGP) is treated as a strongly interacting fluid. Modeling the initial condition of this system has predominantly been done in terms of energy and misses important information about the charges of the quarks. The ICCING (Initial Conserved Charges in Nuclear Geometry) model samples splitting functions to reconstruct the distribution of the conserved charges of the quarks. Differences between the energy initial condition and the distribution of conserved charges provides a way to probe different structures in the QGP.
